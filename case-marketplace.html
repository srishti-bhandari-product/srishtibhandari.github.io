Case Study

Making Resale Feel Effortless

Building for a U.S. recommerce market projected to reach $306.5B by 2030 by making resale feel clear, credible, and easy to choose.

A resale experience only works when it feels obvious, not hidden.

The problem beneath the problem

Resale is not just a supply problem. It is a trust problem.

Customers may want value, but they do not want uncertainty. They want to know what they are buying, why it is worth it, and whether they will be protected if something goes wrong.

The opportunity was to make resale feel like a natural part of shopping, not a separate behavior customers had to learn.

The product question

How do you surface non-new value inside a mainstream shopping experience without making the customer work harder or trust the platform less?

What made it hard

  • Customers were not searching for resale. Discovery had to meet them inside existing behavior.
  • Condition language was not enough. Customers needed clarity, comparison, and confidence.
  • Trust had to be designed. It could not rely on customers reading every detail or interpreting vague cues.

What I led

I led the resale customer experience strategy across search, item page, pricing presentation, and trust cues. The work required close partnership with Search, Personalization, Pricing, Design, and platform teams.

  • Made resale discoverable inside primary shopping journeys
  • Reframed value through clearer cross-condition comparison
  • Improved condition transparency and education
  • Introduced trust cues that reduced hesitation in high consideration categories
  • Balanced growth, fairness, and customer confidence

The decisions that mattered

Make value visible without making the page noisy

If customers do not see the value quickly, resale stays hidden. If the page becomes too dense, the experience becomes harder to trust. The work was about making the right cues visible at the right moment.

Move from condition-first to value-first

Historically, new condition often wins by default. We explored ways to surface the most relevant value across conditions when the experience quality held.

Treat trust as a product surface

Trust is not just policy. It is information architecture. Condition clarity, seller credibility, return reassurance, and savings context all had to work together.

What changed

  • Before: resale was available but easy to miss.
  • After: resale became easier to discover within the primary shopping journey.
  • Before: value required interpretation.
  • After: savings and condition context became easier to compare.
  • Before: trust cues were implicit.
  • After: trust became a designed part of the experience.
