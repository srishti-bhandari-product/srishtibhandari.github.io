Case Study

Making Resale Feel Effortless

Designing a resale experience that makes non-new options feel clear, credible, and easier to choose.

Resale only works when value and trust show up before hesitation does.

The problem beneath the problem

Resale is not just a supply problem. It is a trust problem.

Customers may want value, but they do not want uncertainty. They want to understand what they are buying, why it is worth it, and whether they will be protected if something goes wrong.

The opportunity was to make resale feel like a natural part of shopping, not a separate behavior customers had to learn.

The product challenge

Surface non-new value inside a mainstream shopping experience without making customers work harder or trust the platform less.

What made it hard

  • Customers were not necessarily searching for resale. Discovery had to meet them inside existing shopping behavior.
  • Condition language was not enough. Customers needed clarity, comparison, and confidence.
  • Trust had to be designed. It could not depend on customers reading every detail or interpreting vague cues.

What I led

I led product strategy for the resale customer experience across discovery, item presentation, value communication, and trust cues.

The work required close partnership across search, personalization, pricing, design, and platform teams. The goal was not to make resale louder. It was to make it easier to understand at the moment a customer was already making a decision.

  • Made resale discoverable inside primary shopping journeys.
  • Reframed value through clearer cross-condition comparison.
  • Improved condition transparency and customer education.
  • Introduced trust cues that reduced hesitation in higher-consideration categories.
  • Balanced growth, fairness, and customer confidence.

The decisions that mattered

Make value visible without making the page noisy

If customers do not see the value quickly, resale stays hidden. If the page becomes too dense, the experience becomes harder to trust. The work was about making the right cues visible at the right moment.

Move from condition-first to value-first

New condition often wins by default because it requires less interpretation. We explored ways to surface value across conditions when the experience quality held.

Treat trust as a product surface

Trust is not just policy. It is information architecture. Condition clarity, seller credibility, return reassurance, and savings context all had to work together.

What changed

The experience shifted resale from something available but easy to miss into something easier to discover, compare, and understand within the primary shopping journey.

Value became easier to interpret. Condition context became easier to compare. Trust became a designed part of the experience instead of an assumption.
